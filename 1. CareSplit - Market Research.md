## **Executive Summary: The Nigerian Healthcare Financing Landscape**

Nigeria’s healthcare system is fundamentally characterised by a systemic reliance on out-of-pocket (OOP) payments. This financing model lacks robust risk protection, resulting in "catastrophic health expenditures" for an estimated 150 million people, with roughly 100 million being pushed into poverty annually.

#### **1\. The Critical State of Out-of-Pocket (OOP) Financing**

OOP payments remain the dominant healthcare financing mechanism in Nigeria due to the gross insufficiency of pooled financing arrangements like insurance.

* **Market Share:** Approximately **75% to 77%** of total health expenditure is financed directly by households.  
* **Sector Extremes:** In certain segments, OOP spending has been recorded as high as **81.92%**.  
* **Lack of Protection:** Millions of Nigerians lack financial risk protection, necessitating full payment at the point of service.  
* **Inequity:** The current system is regressive, restricting access for the vulnerable and occasionally impoverishing even wealthier households.

#### **2\. Core Market Challenges and Behavioural Impacts**

The reliance on lump-sum, upfront payments creates severe barriers to medical and financial stability.

**A. Socio-Economic Consequences**

* **The "Debt Trap":** Existing alternatives often involve high-interest medical loans (4.5%+ monthly), leading to significant "debt shame".  
* **Impoverishment:** OOP payments push an estimated **1.2 million Nigerians into poverty** annually.  
* **Catastrophic Thresholds:** A household suffers "catastrophic" expenditure if medical costs exceed **40% of non-food spending**. In the lowest socioeconomic quintile, nearly **48.2% of households** face this reality.  
* **Household Trade-offs:** Families frequently sacrifice basic needs, food, shelter, or education; to meet medical bills.

**B. Impact on Clinical Treatment Behaviour**

* **Medical Suppression:** Patients often convince themselves they do not need care simply because they cannot afford it.  
* **Delayed Presentation:** Anticipated costs lead to treatment delays, resulting in preventable, life-threatening complications.  
* **Treatment Interruption:** For chronic conditions like hypertension, patients skip follow-ups or fail medication cycles due to cash flow issues.  
* **Patronage of Unqualified Providers:** To save costs, many resort to cheaper, unqualified informal providers or traditional healers.

#### **3\. Prevailing Coping Mechanisms**

When faced with medical bills, Nigerians utilise several "economic shocks":

* **Personal Savings:** Used by **47%** of individuals, though often insufficient.  
* **Family/Social Support:** **13%** rely on family networks.  
* **Asset Liquidation:** **4%** sell material assets such as land, livestock, or jewellery.  
* **Labor Mobilization:** Households may remove children from school or mobilise family members into labour to satisfy creditors.
